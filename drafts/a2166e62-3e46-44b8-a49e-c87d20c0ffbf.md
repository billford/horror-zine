"The lucky ones die first."

That tagline hangs over the 2006 remake of *The Hills Have Eyes* like a bad haircut. It’s the sort of promise that usually belongs to grindhouse cinemas or French nouvelle vague shockers, not a Fox Searchlight release. But there it is, stamped on the poster: the tagline for Alexandre Aja’s reboot of the Wes Craven classic.

The movie is a $15 million gamble that somehow managed to return about $70 million, a tidy profit  that speaks to a certain hunger for visceral, unapologetic violence in the mid-2000s.

If you are looking for the slow-burn dread of Craven’s 1977 original, you’re going to be disappointed. Aja, fresh from the chaotic bloodbath of *High Tension*, isn't interested in atmosphere; he’s interested in kinetic energy. He treats the desert not as a backdrop for isolation, but as a literal meat grinder.

The setup is deceptively simple: The Carter family—dad Bob (Ted Levine), mom Ethel (Kathleen Quinlan), daughter Lynn (Vinessa Shaw), son Doug (Aaron Stanford), daughter Brenda (Emilie de Ravin), and baby Catherine—goes on a road trip to California. They break down. They end up in a desolate atomic zone. The mutants come out.

Here is where Aja flips the script on the standard survivalist trope. In *Deliverance*, the city folk are the prey and the locals are the monsters. In *The Hills Have Eyes*, the mutants—the survivors of the atomic zone, the "Deserters," if you want to get all post-apocalyptic about it—are the hunters. The Carters are the invaders, the unwanted pests who have wandered into their territory.

This inversion is the movie’s strongest card, even if it doesn't always play it out logically. The mutants aren't just mindless beasts; they have a tribal structure. Papa Jupiter (Billy Drago) is the alpha, a man whose physical and mental decline has made him a terrifying, shamanistic figure. He’s flanked by Pluto, Ruby, and Lizard, all grotesque products of their environment, but they aren't mindless. They communicate. They strategize.

This is where the makeup effects from Greg Nicotero come into play. Nicotero, a name synonymous with practical horror effects, doesn't just paint people; he sculpts biology. The mutations aren't just lumps of clay; they look like they are growing, rotting, and festering simultaneously. The practical effects give the film a tactile weight that CGI slaughter can rarely replicate. When a head is blown off or a jaw is shattered, you feel the impact because you can see the mechanics of the latex and the blood-spraying apparatus.

The performances are a mixed bag, but Ted Levine is the standout. He carries the film with a charisma that makes you almost root for the cannibals, if only because the family is so relentlessly annoying. Levine, best known for playing the monster in *Silence of the Lambs*, is terrifying not because he roars, but because he’s a man with a job to do. He delivers his lines with a calm, predatory efficiency that is genuinely unsettling.

The rest of the family functions as fodder. They scream, they run, and occasionally they remember they have guns. The film indulges in the classic slasher logic where stupidity is the primary cause of death, but it does so with such frenetic pacing that you forgive them.

The cinematography by Maxime Alexandre is often washed out and dusty, reflecting the arid landscape. The colors are muted browns and oranges, which makes the bursts of blood stand out like neon. It’s a visual style that screams "indie horror" despite the major studio budget.

The script, co-written by Grégoire Levasseur, is functional pulp. It moves the plot from A to B to C to Gore. There’s little subtext, no deep commentary on the human condition, and certainly no pretension. It is exactly what the title suggests: people trapped in eyes, screaming.

Is it a masterpiece? God, no. It lacks the genuine dread of the original. The original film used the isolation of the desert to get inside your head; this version tries to get splatter on your shirt. It’s less psychological horror and more "road trip disaster movie."

However, for what it is—a mid-budget 2000s slasher—*The Hills Have Eyes* is a triumph of energy. It doesn't wait for you to get comfortable. It immediately throws you into the wreckage of the RV and starts knocking on the door.

The sequel, *The Hills Have Eyes 2*, would follow a group of soldiers, but it was a narrative dead end. The franchise would eventually return to the mutants for a direct sequel, *The Hills Have Eyes: The Beginning*, which was set in the 1970s. The continuity is a mess, and the lore is thin, but that doesn't matter when you are watching a man named Papa Jupiter eat a human heart.

Ultimately, the 2006 reboot stands as a testament to the power of style over substance. It’s a loud, ugly, and occasionally brilliant movie about people being hunted in the desert. It respects the gore, it respects the genre, and it respects the audience enough not to treat them like idiots, even if it spends most of its runtime proving otherwise.

It’s a summer movie that works best in the dark, with the sound cranked up, and with a bag of popcorn within arm's reach. The lucky ones die first, indeed. It’s the only way to survive the credits.
